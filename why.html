this is reso.

A personal canvas for every song, lyric, and playlist that shaped you. Not a rating system. Not another streaming app. The place where your music lives as memory.

your music has a story. reso gives it a home.

Every song you love came with a moment. A road trip. A heartbreak. A first time hearing it live. But there's nowhere to save what that song actually means to you. Playlists don't capture it. Star ratings definitely don't. Social media isn't built for that kind of honesty.

Reso is the canvas. A place where your music exists as memories, not metrics. Where the atomic unit is a story, not a score. Where you can be anonymous about the heartbreak playlist and public about the concert photo.

how it works.

You create memory cards -- a song paired with your photo, your story, and the lyric that hit you. Each card gets its own privacy setting: public, friends-only, private, or anonymous. Your page is your canvas. Nobody tells you how to fill it.

Import your Spotify library. The songs are already there. You just add the stories behind them -- when you're ready, at your own pace.

what makes reso different.

🎴

Memory cards, not reviews

Each card is a song + photo + story + lyric. It's not about rating music. It's about what a song means to you.

🔒

Per-card privacy

Every card gets its own visibility. This one's public. This one's just for you. This one's anonymous because the story is too personal to attach your name to. You decide.

🎭

Anonymous sharing

Post the heartbreak playlist without anyone knowing it's yours. Some stories need to be told without a name attached. Reso lets you.

🎵

Spotify import

Connect Spotify and pull in your playlists, top tracks, recently played, and liked songs. Your music library becomes the starting point for your canvas.

🌍

Explore & resonate

Browse what others have shared. When a card hits you, you resonate with it. No likes, no comments, no clout -- just "I felt that too."

four things music actually means to a person.

identity

your taste is who you are.

Your music taste isn't preference. It's identity. The songs you love shape how you see yourself and how you want the world to see you. Your playlist is more honest than your bio will ever be.

Music activates the medial prefrontal cortex -- the region tied to self-identity -- more intensely than food or drugs. "Music you love isn't just rewarding. It feels like ours."
memory

songs are time machines.

One song and you're back in the car on that road trip. You're back in the stadium. You're back in the room with that person. It's involuntary. It's instant. It's not metaphor -- it's what your brain actually does.

The hippocampus attaches music to place, time, and emotion simultaneously. One song encodes an entire era. Music from ages 14-24 is encoded differently -- it's the soundtrack of becoming a person.
emotion

the safe container for things too big to say.

A heartbreak playlist isn't masochistic. It's processing. Slow, sad music doesn't cause sadness -- it produces comfort, nostalgia, and emotional release. The anonymous playlist at 2am is therapy in a form nobody judges.

Studies show sad music triggers prolactin release -- the same hormone involved in comfort and consolation. Listening to sad music when you're sad is your brain asking for what it needs.
belonging

proof that someone else felt it too.

From tribal drums to national anthems to 50,000 people singing the same lyric at a festival -- music builds collective identity. The moment you find out someone loves the same obscure track, you trust them differently.

Music synchronizes neural activity across listeners. Shared musical experiences create social bonds as strong as shared hardship. That's not romantic -- it's neuroscience.
